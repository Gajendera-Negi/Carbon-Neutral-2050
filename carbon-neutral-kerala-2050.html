Carbon Neutral Kerala 2050

About this report

Kerala’s journey towards a carbon-neutral future begins with a bold vision: to become one of the first states in India to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, two decades ahead of the nation’s net-zero target of 2070. To chart this ambitious course, the Directorate of Environment and Climate Change (DoECC), Government of Kerala, in partnership with the Vasudha Foundation, developed the Carbon Neutral Kerala 2050 Pathway. Built upon the scientific foundation of Kerala’s State Greenhouse Gas Inventory and guided by the Kerala State Action Plan on Climate Change (SAPCC 2023–30), the pathway tells the story of how the State can transform its economy, energy system, and development trajectory while safeguarding its people and natural resources.

The pathway takes a deep dive into Kerala’s major emitting sectors, examining how emissions have evolved over time and identifying opportunities to reduce them in the decades ahead. It explores the State’s current energy landscape, projects future energy demand across sectors, and lays out a comprehensive decarbonisation strategy tailored to Kerala’s unique circumstances. At the heart of this vision lies the electricity sector, where a dedicated analysis outlines the steps needed to meet Kerala’s target of sourcing 100% of its electricity from renewable energy by 2040. Achieving this goal will require a profound transformation of the State’s energy system, coupled with significant emissions reductions across transport, buildings, industry, agriculture, and waste management.

Using a bottom-up energy system modelling framework, the study compares a Business-as-Usual (BAU) future with a Carbon Neutral (CN50) pathway, illustrating how different choices made today will shape Kerala’s emissions and energy demand through to 2050. By mapping these alternative futures, the pathway not only highlights the scale of the challenge but also demonstrates that a sustainable, resilient, and carbon-neutral Kerala is achievable. It provides a clear roadmap of sectoral actions, technology transitions, and policy interventions needed to create a cleaner energy future and position Kerala as a leader in climate action and green development.

Gross GHG Emissions in Kerala — BAU vs CN50 Scenarios

Kerala's gross GHG emissions stood at 22.18 MtCO₂e in 2021 and under the BAU scenario, are projected to reach 25.90 MtCO₂e by 2050, reflecting a 16.77% increase under current trends in energy use, waste management, and other sectors. Emissions are expected to peak around the year 2030 at 26.70 MtCO₂e before gradually declining towards 2050 within the BAU trajectory. The energy sector is projected to drive the majority of this increase between 2021 and 2050 (3.18 MtCO₂e or 85.37% of the total rise). Within energy, transport is 63.2% of emissions; buildings 21.6%; industry incl. captive power 14.5%. Through dedicated interventions aimed at electrification, fuel switching and energy efficiency across energy sectors, and improved waste management practices, as much as 55.54% of the projected gross emissions in BAU could be abated.

Multi-fold increase in the electricity demand by 2050; therefore, higher integration of renewable sources would be required to meet this demand.

Kerala’s transition from a fossil fuel-based economy to an electrified future will significantly increase electricity demand, which is projected to reach 123.70 TWh by 2050—almost five times the 2021 level. Industry and transport will drive much of this growth, while rising cooling needs in buildings will be partly offset by energy-efficient technologies. To meet this demand with 100% renewable electricity, the state will require 65.06 GW of renewable energy capacity by 2050. Building on its strong rooftop solar performance, Kerala will need to expand utility-scale solar capacity to 50.52 GW and wind capacity to 11.28 GW, laying the foundation for a fully renewable energy future.

Solar-Dominated Renewable Growth Drives cleaner power portfolio mix by 2050

By 2050, the power sector is almost fully decarbonised in both the BAU and CN50 scenarios, with renewable energy, including large hydro, supplying over 95% of total electricity generation. Solar emerges as the dominant source, contributing nearly 70–73% of generation, supported by significant additions from wind and hydropower. Coal generation declines to less than 1% of total supply, remaining only to meet contractual obligations, while a small share of electricity is procured from inter-state markets. As a result, the grid emission factor falls sharply from about 0.58 kgCO₂/kWh in 2021 to near-zero levels by 2045 in BAU and by 2040 in CN50, reflecting the state's transition toward a predominantly renewable electricity system.

Deep electrification across energy sectors will act as a strategic enabler for economy wide decarbonisation.

The CN50 scenario tells a larger story than emissions reduction—it redefines energy security. Today, energy security depends heavily on access to global oil and gas markets, leaving economies exposed to price volatility, supply disruptions, and geopolitical risks. Deep electrification changes this dynamic by shifting energy demand to domestically generated renewable electricity. In transport, the pathway envisions 100% electrification of new two- and three-wheelers by 2040, followed by near-complete electrification of passenger vehicles and light goods vehicles by 2050, with some diesel use remaining in heavy trucks. In buildings, the adoption of electric cookstoves across around 55 lakh households and commercial establishments reduces dependence on LPG. Industries also increasingly replace fossil-fuel-based heating with electric alternatives wherever feasible. Together, these transitions strengthen energy independence, reduce exposure to global fuel markets, and could collectively abate about 11.35 MtCO₂e annually by 2050.

While the evolving economic and technical landscape may impact the pace of action vis-a-vis interventions proposed under the CN50 scenario, carbon neutrality would still remain achievable.

The CN50 scenario charts Kerala’s path to carbon neutrality through ambitious interventions across energy and non-energy sectors, including full electrification of new two- and three-wheelers by 2040, electric four-wheelers by 2050, widespread adoption of electric cookstoves, and enhanced carbon sequestration through fallow land conversion. While economic and technological uncertainties may affect implementation, Kerala could still achieve carbon neutrality ahead of its 2050 target, potentially by 2045.

Sensitivity analysis shows that even under less favorable conditions—such as slower electrification in transport and industry or lower sequestration rates—the transition remains resilient. For example, if only half of the projected transport electrification is realized, the state’s net-negative milestone would shift from 2032 to around 2040, demonstrating the robustness of the CN50 pathway.

Enhancement of sequestration by harnessing the potential of land use and blue carbon capture would be equally paramount – leading to net negative emissions by 2050.

The State’s path to carbon neutrality relies on strengthening natural carbon sinks through forests, agroforestry, social forestry, and coastal ecosystems. By improving forest health and increasing carbon stock densities, the State can enhance carbon sequestration while delivering co-benefits such as better soil health, biodiversity conservation, and sustainable livelihoods. Repurposing 75% of 841.16 sq. km of barren and fallow land for agroforestry and social forestry, along with restoring blue carbon ecosystems such as mangroves, seagrasses, and wetlands, can significantly boost carbon removal. Mangrove cover alone is projected to increase from 21.15 sq. km to 100.60 sq. km, contributing to an estimated total carbon sequestration potential of approximately 23.91 MtCO₂e.

Way forward

The CN50 pathway’s implementation will require coordinated action across sectors, alignment of institutional and financial systems, and sustained stakeholder engagement, while retaining flexibility to respond to emerging technologies and evolving development priorities.

Sectoral departments will play a primary role in designing and executing interventions, supported by integration with existing planning processes. Implementation will follow a sector-led, decentralised approach enabling context-specific planning at the local governance level.

Mobilising public finance, enabling access to climate finance, and facilitating private sector participation are critical. Along with this, an enabling ecosystem for technology adoption and innovation will further be strengthened through improved linkages between research, industry, and implementation systems, supporting effective deployment and diffusion of low-carbon solutions.

A robust approach to monitoring and review will be essential to track progress and guide decision-making, with periodic refinement of strategies to ensure continued alignment with technological, economic, and policy developments.
